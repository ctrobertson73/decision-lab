The Student Who Arrives

A student completing the three-course foundational sequence arrives with a defined professional toolkit. From HLSC 205: AI-assisted SQL querying, statistical test selection and execution, data visualization, and professional AI prompting for analysis. From EXSC 325: movement screening and assessment, practice design (blocked/random, whole/part), feedback intervention design, training load monitoring (ACWR), and skill acquisition tracking. From EXSC 405: injury mechanism understanding, return-to-play protocol reasoning, biomechanical risk assessment, and rehabilitation outcome tracking. Students who also completed EXSC 442 add logistic regression, mixed-effects models, KNN, Random Forest, and model evaluation to that list.

All of this was built on simulated data. The practicum is where they prove it transfers.

Four Placement Models
Option A

Single-Sport Embedded

Student is assigned to one varsity sport for the semester as a Sport Science Intern. They attend practices and competitions, collect and enter performance data, run queries for coaching staff, present at team meetings, and shadow athletic trainers during injury assessments. This model builds deep, sport-specific expertise and the closest professional relationships.

1–3 credits (45–135 hours)
Best for: students who want depth in one program and a strong reference from a single coaching staff
Option B

Rotation Model

Student rotates through three departments (Analytics/Coaching, Strength & Conditioning, Athletic Training), applying each foundational course's professional lens in sequence. The capstone requirement is an integrated case study on 1-2 athletes seen across all three rotations — demonstrating the multi-lens reasoning the entire pipeline was designed to build.

3 credits (135 hours = 45 per rotation)
Best for: students who want breadth and who completed all three foundational courses
Option C

Project-Based

Athletics poses a real research or operational question, and the student conducts the full investigation: proposal, data collection and analysis, written report, staff presentation, and recommendations document. This model mirrors the HLSC 499 thesis but with a tighter operational focus and faster turnaround — the deliverable is a decision tool, not an academic paper.

2–3 credits (90–135 hours)
Best for: students with strong analytical skills (especially 442 completers) who want a portfolio-quality project
Option D

Athlete Case Management

Student is assigned 3-5 athletes to follow longitudinally for the semester. For each athlete they create three deliverables through the three professional lenses: a performance dashboard (205 lens), a motor control profile with training recommendations (325 lens), and an injury risk assessment with prevention plan (405 lens). Weekly duties include athlete check-ins, data updates, staff consultations, and progress notes.

2 credits (90 hours)
Best for: students interested in athletic training or sports medicine who want longitudinal case experience

The rotation model is the structural payoff of the foundational tier. A student who has sat in all three seats at the sport science team table in simulation now rotates through all three departments in practice. Each rotation is supervised by the relevant professional (sport coach, S&C coach, head athletic trainer), and the student applies the specific course lens for that rotation. The integrated case study at the end requires pulling from all three rotations — the same multi-lens synthesis they practiced in NB5 of EXSC 325 and in the multi-table queries of EXSC 405, but now with real athletes and real supervisors reading the output.

Embedded Semester Arc

From orientation through synthesis — one sport, full depth

Weeks 1–2 · Orientation + Baseline Assessments

Meet the coaching staff. Learn the program's culture, terminology, and operational rhythms. Enter baseline movement screening data for the roster. Establish the data collection protocols that will govern the rest of the semester. First deliverable: a clean dataset the coaching staff can reference.

Weeks 3–6 · Training Load Monitoring

Collect and enter real performance data at every practice and competition. Run ACWR calculations and produce weekly training load reports for the coaching staff. Learn to communicate load data in language the coach uses, not the language the textbook uses. Deliverable: weekly ACWR reports delivered directly to the sport coach.

Weeks 7–10 · Performance Tracking

Shift from data entry to data interpretation. Run queries against the accumulating season dataset. Build visualizations that show performance trends, positional comparisons, and individual trajectories. Present a mid-season analysis to the coaching staff — the first time the student stands in front of professionals and defends an interpretation. Deliverable: mid-season performance analysis presentation.

Weeks 11–14 · Intervention Implementation

Design and implement one motor control or performance intervention with 3-5 athletes, drawing from the feedback, practice design, and training load principles of EXSC 325. Track the intervention's effects in the database. This is the Decision Cycle operating at full speed: QUERY (what's the problem?), INTERPRET (what does the data show?), DECIDE (what intervention?), JUSTIFY (what's the theory and the evidence?). Deliverable: intervention tracking data with preliminary results.

Weeks 15–16 · Synthesis

Compile the semester's work into a comprehensive end-of-season performance report for the coaching staff. Present findings, highlight what the data revealed that wasn't obvious from watching practice, and make recommendations for the off-season. Deliverable: end-of-season performance report and exit presentation.

Competency Assessment

Five Demonstrated Competencies

Assessment is competency-based, consistent with every course in the pipeline. Supervisors evaluate professional behavior, analytical quality, and communication effectiveness. Students produce tangible deliverables and present findings to real stakeholders. The standard remains: if you can't explain it, you don't own it.

Competency Demonstration Standard
Data Query & Analysis Generate 5+ actionable analytical reports for coaching or athletic training staff using the shared database and Decision Cycle structure
Motor Control Application Design and implement at least one training intervention grounded in motor learning theory, with documented rationale and tracked outcomes
Injury Risk Assessment Complete movement screening-based risk profiles for assigned athletes with evidence-based prevention recommendations
Professional Communication Present analytical findings to coaches, athletic trainers, or S&C staff at least twice during the semester, defending recommendations under questioning
Ethical Data Handling Maintain FERPA compliance throughout the placement. No athlete data leaves the authorized system. No identifiable information in any deliverable shared outside the athletics department
Supervision & Evaluation
EVAL

Dual Evaluation Model

Faculty Advisor + Site Supervisor

Faculty Advisor (Dr. Robertson)

Sets learning objectives with the student at placement start. Reviews deliverables for analytical quality and Decision Cycle adherence. Conducts mid-semester check-in and final portfolio review. Grades all academic components (reflection journal, final report, exit presentation). Ensures the practicum experience connects back to the foundational coursework rather than becoming untethered task completion.

Site Supervisor (Athletics Staff)

Provides daily operational supervision. Evaluates professional behavior, reliability, communication, and the usefulness of the student's analytical contributions to the program. Completes a formal mid-semester check-in and a final evaluation form. The supervisor evaluation is required but does not carry a letter grade — it is professional feedback that the student includes in their portfolio.

The Partnership Value Exchange

What Students Get

Real experience for a resume. A professional network within collegiate athletics. Portfolio pieces with actual outcomes attached. Potential job or graduate assistantship references from coaches and athletic trainers who have watched them work. And a documented competency record showing they have done the job — not in a classroom exercise, not in a simulated scenario, but for real people making real decisions about real athletes.

What Athletics Gets

Skilled analytical labor — students who can query the database, run reports, and produce visualizations that the staff doesn't have time to build. A fresh perspective on their own data. A pipeline of trained students who understand the program's systems and could return as graduate assistants. And a culture of data-informed practice that accumulates over semesters as each cohort builds on the previous one's work.

Pedagogical Framework

Decision Cycle as Professional Habit

In HLSC 205, the Decision Cycle was a learning scaffold — students used it because the assignment required it. In EXSC 325, it became the structure for coaching prescriptions. In EXSC 405, it governed clinical reasoning. In HLSC 499, students executed it independently on a real research question. Here in HLSC 490, the Decision Cycle is no longer an assignment structure. It is a professional habit. Students Query before they analyze, Interpret before they recommend, Decide with specificity, and Justify under the gaze of coaches who will implement (or reject) their recommendation. The cycle has moved from scaffolding to instinct.

AI in Professional Context

Students bring their AI-as-labor-partner workflow into the placement. They use Claude and other tools to generate code, troubleshoot queries, and draft report structures — the same way a working sport science analyst would. The difference from coursework is that the output has an immediate audience with immediate consequences. A dashboard built for a coach is reviewed by that coach, not by Dr. Robertson. A training load report presented at a team meeting is questioned by professionals who care about the answer, not about the method. The AI boundary is enforced by the professional context itself: if the student can't explain what their tool produced, the coach won't trust it.

The Simulated-to-Real Completion

The entire pipeline was designed to make this transition feel natural rather than overwhelming. Students have worked with the same database schema, the same Decision Cycle, the same professional role structure, and the same AI tools across every foundational course. The only variable that changed was the stakes. In the practicum, the stakes are fully real — but the infrastructure is familiar. A student who built a performance dashboard in HLSC 205, designed a coaching intervention in EXSC 325, and defended a thesis for JU Athletics in HLSC 499 already knows the work. In HLSC 490, they simply do it every day.

The Culmination

Where the Pipeline Ends — and Where the Career Begins

HLSC 490 is the final station in the Sport Science Decision Lab. Students who complete it have occupied every seat at the sport science team table: data analyst, performance coach, sports medicine clinician, applied researcher, and professional practitioner. They have queried the same data from five professional perspectives and made decisions with progressively higher stakes across their entire undergraduate career. They leave with a portfolio, a professional network, demonstrated competencies, and the one thing no classroom can provide: the experience of having done the work for someone who needed the answer.

The pipeline positions graduates for entry into sport science, athletic training, strength and conditioning, sports medicine, and healthcare data analytics roles across Florida's workforce. But more than career preparation, it has trained a specific professional capacity: the ability to encounter ambiguous data, determine what it means for a real person, defend that interpretation to a stakeholder, and accept responsibility for the recommendation. That capacity does not expire with graduation. It is the foundation of every analytical decision they will make for the rest of their career.
