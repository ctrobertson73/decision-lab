01

Data Literacy & Descriptive Analysis

Weeks 1–4 · Building the Analytical Foundation

The Entry Point

Students meet the sport science database and learn to ask questions of it. This unit establishes the complete tool chain (Excel, Python via Google Colab, AI assistants) and the foundational skill of data literacy: understanding what variables you have, what they measure, how to describe their distributions, and how to communicate those descriptions visually. No inferential claims yet — students learn to see before they learn to test.

Sport Science Context

You've been given access to the JU Athletics performance database. Before you run a single test, what do you need to know about the data? Who are these athletes? What was measured? What does the distribution of training loads look like across the roster?

Students build their first AI-assisted SQL queries against simulated athletic performance data. They learn that the quality of every analysis downstream depends on the quality of the question asked here — and that querying is a reasoning task, not a technical one. The Decision Cycle is introduced: QUERY (what do we need to know?), INTERPRET (what does the data show us?), DECIDE (what does this mean?), JUSTIFY (why should anyone trust this summary?).

Topics

Database orientation and AI-assisted SQL querying
Variables, sampling, and measurement scales
Data collection and import (Excel + Python)
Central tendency (mean, median, mode)
Variability (SD, range, IQR)
Distributions and shape
Data visualization (histograms, boxplots, scatter)
Decision Cycle introduction

Probability & Distributions

Weeks 5–7 · The Mathematical Foundations

Why Probability Matters

Before students can test a hypothesis, they need to understand what makes an observation unusual. This unit builds the probability scaffolding that underlies every inferential test: what distributions describe, how probability quantifies uncertainty, and why the normal distribution is the engine behind most of the tests they'll use. The unit is condensed and rigorous — probability basics and distribution concepts are paired in single sessions to maintain momentum.

Sport Science Context

An athlete's ACWR is 1.45. Is that unusual? Unusual compared to what? The answer depends entirely on what distribution of ACWR values you're comparing against — and understanding that comparison is the foundation of every statistical test you'll run.

Topics

Probability basics and rules
Distributions: conceptual introduction
Normal distribution: properties and applications
T-distribution
Chi-square distribution
Binomial and Poisson distributions
Z-scores and standardization
Connecting distributions to real athletic data

03

Inference Foundations

Weeks 8–10 · From Data to Conclusions

The Reasoning Framework

This unit bridges description and decision. Students learn the logic of statistical inference: sampling distributions, the Central Limit Theorem, confidence intervals, and hypothesis testing. JASP is introduced as a tool for running analyses with a visual, menu-driven interface, complementing the code-based work in Python. The critical distinction between statistical significance and practical significance is introduced here and will recur in every subsequent course in the pipeline.

Sport Science Context

You've measured vertical jump height for 25 athletes before and after a six-week plyometric program. The mean improved by 2.1 cm. Is that real improvement or just noise? How confident are you? And even if it's statistically significant, does a 2.1 cm improvement actually matter to a coach?

Topics

Sampling distributions and the Central Limit Theorem
Confidence intervals: construction and interpretation
JASP introduction for statistical analysis
Hypothesis testing: null, alternative, p-values
Type I and Type II errors
Effect sizes: Cohen's d, practical significance
Statistical vs. practical significance
Decision Cycle applied to hypothesis testing

04

Statistical Tests

Weeks 10–12 · The Analytical Toolkit

One Test Per Session

Each statistical test gets its own dedicated session. This is deliberate: rather than surveying tests in a blur, students encounter each one as a tool with a specific purpose, specific assumptions, and specific output to interpret. Every session follows the same structure: what question does this test answer, when do you use it instead of the others, what does the output mean, and how do you report it for a stakeholder? The End-of-Semester Project is launched at the end of this unit — students choose which test to use for their scouting report, and that choice is the first real Decision Cycle they own.

Sport Science Context

Is there a significant difference in sprint times between athletes who completed the off-season training program and those who didn't? Is position correlated with injury rate? Does vertical jump predict in-game performance? Each question demands a different test — and choosing the wrong one produces an answer to a question nobody asked.

Tests (One Per Session)

t-tests: paired and independent samples
ANOVA: comparing multiple groups
Correlation: strength and direction of association
Regression: linear and multiple
Chi-square: categorical associations
Test selection logic: matching question to method

The Selection Problem

This unit is where students confront the core challenge of applied statistics: choosing the right tool. AI can execute any test instantly. The analyst's job is to know which test answers the question being asked and why the alternatives don't. That judgment — which method fits this question — is the skill that carries forward to every course in the pipeline, from the motor control interventions of EXSC 325 to the predictive models of EXSC 442 to the thesis investigations of HLSC 499.

End-of-Semester Project · The JU Athletics Scouting Report

Weeks 13–14 (Asynchronous) + Live Defense (Final Exam Block, Nov 20)

The Commission

JU's Athletics Director has formally commissioned the Data Analytics unit to produce a scouting report. The student is the data analyst. The director has a question about one of the varsity programs — recruiting, positional fit, performance trends, injury patterns, training load, team comparisons — and needs the answer in language she can act on. She does not read SQL. She does not read code. She reads conclusions, evidence, and recommendations.

The Process

Week 13 (Commission Check-In): Submit the research question, stakeholder, statistical approach, and data plan. Dr. Robertson approves, sharpens, or returns for revision. NotebookLM consultation required before submission: students use AI to find the weakest point in their plan.

Week 14 (Analysis + Report): Build the complete analysis in Google Colab, organized by Decision Cycle headers (QUERY, INTERPRET, DECIDE, JUSTIFY). Write the 1-2 page scouting report for the Athletics Director with presentation-quality visualizations embedded in the document.

Final Exam Block (Live Defense): 5-8 minutes. Present directly from report and notebook. Defend every phase of the Decision Cycle under questioning, including the AI boundary probe: "Show me where you used AI and tell me exactly what you decided was wrong with its first suggestion."

Defense Probes

Why this question? Who benefits from the answer?
Walk me through your method. Why this test?
Explain this visualization to the Athletics Director.
Statistical vs. practical significance — which did you find?
State your recommendation in one sentence.
I'm a coach who disagrees. Convince me.
What's the biggest limitation of your analysis?
Where did you use AI, and what did you override?

What This Proves

A student who passes this defense has demonstrated that they can occupy the Data Analyst seat at the sport science team table. They can query data, interpret it, decide on a recommendation, and justify it under questioning. This is the analytical engine that powers everything else in the pipeline.

Pedagogical Framework

Water Pedagogy: Encounter Before Explanation

There are no dedicated practice or review sessions. Every content session includes exploration and application — students encounter the problem before receiving the framework that names it. Competencies arrive without rehearsal. This is deliberate: the encounter-first model builds the tolerance for ambiguity that professional practice demands. A sport science data analyst doesn't get a practice round before the coach asks for the report.

AI Integration: From Day One

Students have access to Claude and NotebookLM from the first session. AI tools are used to generate SQL queries, write Python code, troubleshoot errors, and explore alternative approaches. The principle is consistent and explicit: AI does the labor, the student owns the thinking. The AI boundary probe in every competency and the final defense tests this directly. If a student cannot explain what their AI-generated code does, they do not pass.

Competency-Based, Pass/Fail Assessment

Two in-class competencies plus the End-of-Semester Project. All pass/fail. No exams. No partial credit. Students demonstrate understanding under questioning, in the moment, without a net. C01 gates descriptive analysis. C02 gates probability and inference. The project gates the full Decision Cycle applied to a self-chosen question. The standard is the same in every course in the pipeline: if you can't explain it, you don't own it.

Where This Leads

The Analytical Engine for the Entire Pipeline

Students who pass HLSC 205 carry forward the ability to query data, describe it, test hypotheses about it, and communicate findings to stakeholders. In EXSC 325, the question shifts from "what does the data say?" to "what should we do about it?" — the same database, the same tools, but now the output is a coaching prescription rather than a scouting report. In EXSC 442, the methods become predictive rather than inferential. In EXSC 405, the reasoning applies to clinical anatomy and injury. In HLSC 499, the data becomes real. In HLSC 490, the analysis becomes daily professional practice. What students build here — the querying habits, the Decision Cycle fluency, the AI-as-labor-partner workflow — is the foundation that every subsequent course depends on.
