Athlete Profiling & Baseline Data

Weeks 1–2 · Who Is This Athlete?

The Starting Point

Before a performance coach can prescribe anything, they need to know who they're working with. This notebook teaches students to build an athlete profile from the database: movement screening scores, proprioceptive baselines, feedback control characteristics, and the athlete's position within their team's roster. Students encounter the database again from HLSC 205, but now the question isn't "what does the data describe?" — it's "what does this data tell me about how this athlete moves and learns?"

Sport Science Context

You've been assigned a volleyball outside hitter. Her FMS score is 14, her Y-Balance composite is 94%, and her movement screening flagged a right-side asymmetry. Before you design anything, what does this profile tell you about her motor control system?

02

Tracking Skill Acquisition

Weeks 3–5 · How Do Athletes Learn?

The Learning Science

Athletes don't improve linearly. They plateau, regress, break through, and consolidate. This notebook gives students the theoretical frameworks to understand those patterns and the data tools to track them. Fitts and Posner's stages of motor learning provide the classification scheme. Power law learning curves provide the mathematical shape. Variability analysis reveals whether an athlete's inconsistency is noise (bad) or exploration (good, depending on the stage). Python is introduced here as the analytical tool for longitudinal tracking.

Sport Science Context

A basketball player's free throw percentage has been flat at 68% for six weeks despite daily practice. Is she plateaued, stuck in the associative stage, or has the practice structure stopped challenging her motor system? The data can tell you — if you know what to look for.

Topics

Stages of motor learning (Fitts & Posner)
Neural basis of learning and consolidation
Learning curves, plateaus, and power law
Python introduction for longitudinal tracking
Variability analysis: noise vs. exploration
Stage classification from performance data

03

Practice Design & Training Load

Weeks 5–7 · How Should We Train?

The Design Problem

This is where motor control theory becomes coaching prescription. Students learn that how you structure practice matters as much as what you practice: blocked practice looks productive but produces fragile skills; random practice looks messy but builds durable transfer. The Challenge Point Framework provides the optimization principle: the difficulty of the practice environment should match the learner's skill level. And training load monitoring (ACWR) provides the safety constraint: push too hard and you break the athlete, push too little and you waste their time.

Sport Science Context

A soccer midfielder's ACWR is 1.35 and climbing. Her coach wants to add a new set-piece routine to practice. The Challenge Point Framework says she's ready for complexity. The load data says she's approaching the danger zone. You have to make a recommendation the coach can act on — what do you prescribe, and what's the motor learning rationale?

Topics

Practice structure: blocked vs. random
Contextual interference effect
Challenge Point Framework
Training load monitoring and ACWR
Neural fatigue and recovery
Fitts' Law and speed-accuracy tradeoffs

04

Feedback & Intervention Tracking

Weeks 7–9 · How Do We Guide Improvement?

The Intervention Science

The most common coaching instinct — give more feedback, more often, more specifically — is wrong for most stages of learning. This notebook builds the evidence base for how, when, and what kind of feedback actually produces durable skill change. External attentional focus cues outperform internal cues. Faded feedback schedules outperform constant feedback. The guidance hypothesis explains why: feedback that's too frequent becomes a crutch the motor system leans on rather than a signal it internalizes. Students design feedback interventions, track their effectiveness in the database, and learn to prescribe changes based on data rather than intuition.

Sport Science Context

A pitching coach has been giving detailed mechanical feedback after every throw. The pitcher's form looks great during practice but falls apart in games. The data shows a classic guidance dependency pattern. How do you restructure the feedback schedule without the coach feeling like you're telling them to stop coaching?

Topics

Attentional focus: internal vs. external cues
Sensory integration and feedback channels
Knowledge of results vs. knowledge of performance
Guidance hypothesis and faded feedback
Feedback effectiveness analysis from data
Intervention design and prescription
⬥ Competency 02 — Week 9: Practice design and feedback intervention under questioning

Integrated Performance Monitoring

Weeks 10–12 · The Whole Athlete

The Integration Challenge

Real performance coaching doesn't happen in isolated domains. An athlete's transfer problem might be caused by a practice structure issue (NB3), a feedback dependency (NB4), a load spike (NB3), or a stage mismatch (NB2) — or some combination. This final notebook synthesizes every framework into integrated case analysis. Students query across the full database — screening data, learning curves, load history, feedback records, and competition performance — to build comprehensive athlete assessments that pull from all four prior notebooks.

Sport Science Context

A track sprinter's 200m times have regressed 0.3 seconds over the past month despite consistent training. Is this fatigue (check ACWR), a technique breakdown under pressure (check dual-task data), a plateau requiring practice restructuring (check learning curve shape), or loss of automaticity from too much conscious attention (check reinvestment indicators)? The answer requires pulling from every notebook in this course.

Topics

Transfer and retention testing
Automaticity and dual-task assessment
Performance under pressure and reinvestment
Multi-table database synthesis
Comprehensive case studies
Performance-learning paradox

The Key Insight

The performance-learning paradox is the organizing concept of this notebook and the deepest motor control insight students take forward: what looks like good performance in practice (high accuracy, low variability) often predicts poor learning (fragile skills, no transfer). And what looks like poor performance in practice (errors, variability, struggle) often predicts durable learning. A coach who doesn't understand this paradox will design practice that looks productive but isn't. A performance coach who does understand it can explain to a skeptical head coach why the messy practice is actually working — and back it up with data.

End-of-Semester Project · The Coaching Consultation

Weeks 13–14 (Asynchronous) + Live Defense (Final Exam Block, Nov 23)

The Commission

A JU head coach has formally commissioned the Sport Science Performance unit to produce a motor control consultation for one of their athletes. The student is the performance coach. The coach has a struggling athlete — a skill that isn't transferring, a fragile movement breaking under pressure, a practice plan that should be working but isn't — and needs a recommendation they can act on by next week. The coach understands sport. The coach does not read variability indices, ACWR, dual-task cost, or Fitts' Law. The coach reads recommendations, evidence, and rationale.

The Process

Week 13 (Commission Check-In): Submit the athlete, research question, motor control lens, and data plan. The athlete must not have been analyzed in a previous class session. NotebookLM consultation required: students use AI to identify the weakest point in their approach and the mismatch they might not be seeing.

Week 14 (Analysis + Report): Build the complete analysis in Google Colab with Decision Cycle headers. Produce a 1-2 page coaching consultation with at least two visualizations showing the athlete in context (against teammates or their own history). The coach should be able to understand the finding from the visuals alone.

Final Exam Block (Live Defense): 5-8 minutes. Present directly from report and notebook. Defend every phase of the Decision Cycle including theory-to-data connections and the AI boundary probe.

Defense Probes

Why this athlete? What made their data worth investigating?
What motor control concept is this finding an example of?
Explain this visualization as if you're talking to the head coach.
State your recommendation in one sentence.
I'm the coach. I disagree because it doesn't look game-like. Convince me.
Explain the performance-learning paradox as it applies here.
If this didn't work by Friday, what's your first move?
Where did you use AI, and what did you override?

What This Proves

A student who passes this defense has demonstrated that they can occupy the Performance Coach seat at the sport science team table. They can profile an athlete, identify a motor control problem in the data, design an evidence-based intervention, and defend that prescription to the coach who has to implement it. The question has shifted from "what does the data say?" (the 205 seat) to "what should we do about it?" — same database, same Decision Cycle, different professional judgment.

Pedagogical Framework

The Notebook Structure

The course is organized into five notebooks rather than traditional units. Each notebook is a self-contained analytical environment in the shared database — students build queries, run analyses, and interpret findings within the notebook's thematic frame. The notebooks accumulate: by NB5, students are pulling from all five data domains simultaneously. This structure mirrors how a performance coach actually works: the athlete's screening data, learning history, training load, feedback response, and competition performance all exist in the same system, and the coach has to synthesize across all of them to make a recommendation.

Theory-to-Data-to-Prescription

Every session in this course follows a three-part logic: name the motor control theory, show the data pattern it predicts, and prescribe the coaching action it supports. Students cannot recommend a practice change without citing the theory behind it and the data that confirms it applies to this athlete. This structure is what separates a performance coach from someone who just runs drills — and it's the structure the Decision Cycle enforces in every competency and in the final defense.

Water Pedagogy and Competency Gates

No practice sessions, no review days. Content sessions run directly into competency days. C01 gates athlete profiling and skill acquisition (NB1-2). C02 gates practice design and feedback (NB3-4). The project integrates all five notebooks. The encounter-first model means students explore motor control phenomena in the data before receiving the theoretical name for what they've found. A student who discovers a learning curve plateau in the data before hearing the term "power law" owns that concept differently than one who memorized it from a textbook.

Where This Leads

From Prescription to Risk Management

In HLSC 205, students learned to describe data and test hypotheses. Here in EXSC 325, they learned to turn data into coaching prescriptions grounded in motor learning theory. The question shifts again in EXSC 405: from optimizing performance to managing risk. Same athletes, same database, but now the consequences of a wrong decision involve injury, rehabilitation, and return-to-play. Students who've occupied both the Data Analyst seat (205) and the Performance Coach seat (325) arrive at the Clinical Anatomist seat (405) understanding that the sport science team table has multiple perspectives, and that no single lens captures the whole picture.

In EXSC 442, the motor control data from this course becomes the input for predictive models: can training load patterns predict injury? Can variability trends classify an athlete's readiness? In HLSC 499, the coaching consultation becomes a real research project for JU Athletics. In HLSC 490, the performance coach role becomes daily professional practice. The theory-to-data-to-prescription logic built here is the analytical habit that every subsequent course depends on.
