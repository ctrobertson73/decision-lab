The Sport Science Decision Lab is built on a single architectural principle: students should encounter the same data, the same reasoning framework, and the same professional role structure across every course in the sequence. The only variable that changes as they progress is the stakes. What begins as simulated encounters with athlete data in the classroom becomes authentic research with JU Athletics, and ultimately supervised professional practice. Each course trains a distinct professional lens — data analyst, performance coach, sports medicine clinician, researcher, practitioner — while the shared Decision Cycle (Query → Interpret → Decide → Justify) and the shared sport science database provide structural coherence across the entire pipeline.

205

Statistics for Health & Exercise Science

Data Literacy & Analytical Reasoning
Professional Lens: Sport Scientist / Data Analyst

Course Description

The entry point to the Decision Lab pipeline. Students encounter the shared sport science database for the first time, learning to query athlete data, select and execute statistical tests, and visualize results for stakeholder communication. Every analytical task is framed through the Decision Cycle: students do not simply run tests — they must articulate why they chose a particular analysis, what the output means in context, and what decision it supports.

AI tools (Claude, NotebookLM) are introduced as labor partners. Students use AI to assist with code generation, data processing, and query construction, but they own the reasoning. The principle is explicit: AI does the labor, the student owns the thinking. No pre-written code or queries appear in student materials — they must construct their own analytical approach, using AI as a collaborator rather than an answer key.

Database Engagement

Students work with simulated athlete records spanning demographics, sport-specific performance metrics, training load data, and assessment scores. The database uses the same schema that will reappear in every subsequent course, so the querying skills built here transfer directly. Students generate custom datasets, write AI-assisted SQL queries, and produce professional data visualizations.

Skills Developed

AI-assisted SQL querying
Descriptive and inferential statistics
Statistical test selection and execution
Data visualization and interpretation
Professional AI prompting for analysis
Effect size interpretation
Decision Cycle reasoning (introduction)
Stakeholder communication of findings

What Students Carry Forward

Graduates of this course arrive at EXSC 325 and EXSC 405 knowing how to extract and analyze data from the sport science database. They can write queries, select appropriate statistical tests, interpret output, and produce visualizations. They have a working relationship with AI tools and understand the boundary between AI labor and their own reasoning. This is the analytical engine that powers everything else in the pipeline.

Students move from extracting and analyzing data to interpreting it for training decisions — same database, different professional questions
EXSC
325

Motor Control

Performance Science & Training Design
Professional Lens: Performance Coach / Strength & Conditioning

Course Description

The analytical skills from HLSC 205 shift from "what does the data say?" to "what should we do about it?" Students engage the same sport science database through the lens of a performance coach, using motor control and motor learning principles to design, evaluate, and justify training decisions. The course is built around the science of how athletes acquire, retain, and transfer skill — and how a coach uses data to guide that process.

Students work with training load monitoring (Acute-to-Chronic Workload Ratio), practice design variables (blocked vs. random, whole vs. part), feedback interventions (external focus cues, faded feedback schedules), and skill acquisition tracking. Every decision must be grounded in both motor learning theory and data from the database — students cannot recommend a training change without querying the evidence and justifying their reasoning through the Decision Cycle.

Database Engagement

The database expands to include movement screening scores (FMS, Y-Balance), skill acquisition logs with progression markers, training load and periodization tracking, and performance benchmarking data. Students query this data to design training progressions, evaluate intervention effectiveness, and make coaching recommendations backed by evidence.

Skills Developed

Movement screening and assessment
Training load monitoring (ACWR)
Practice design (blocked/random, whole/part)
Feedback intervention design
Skill acquisition tracking and analysis
Performance benchmarking
Evidence-based coaching recommendations
Decision Cycle reasoning (applied judgment)

What Students Carry Forward

Students leave this course able to interpret performance data for training decisions — not just describe it, but act on it. They understand the difference between data that describes and data that directs. They can design a training intervention, monitor its effects, and adjust based on evidence. These applied judgment skills set up the clinical reasoning demands of EXSC 405 and the independent research design of HLSC 499.

The reasoning shifts from optimizing performance to managing risk — same athletes, but now the consequences of a wrong decision involve injury and recovery
EXSC
405

Advanced Human Anatomy (Clinical Neuroanatomy)

Sports Medicine & Clinical Reasoning
Professional Lens: Sports Trainer / Physical Therapist

Course Description

The highest-stakes reasoning in the foundational tier. Students work through the lens of a sports medicine clinician — someone who must connect anatomical knowledge to real injury scenarios, return-to-play decisions, and biomechanical risk assessment. The course integrates JU's Anatomage virtual dissection table as a clinical reasoning tool: students don't simply study anatomy on the table, they solve cases by linking the structures they see to the symptoms in front of them.

Sports medicine case scenarios anchor the course. A student receives a case (an athlete with a specific mechanism of injury, a set of symptoms, a competitive timeline), uses the Anatomage table to examine the relevant anatomy, queries the database for injury history and biomechanical risk factors, and produces a clinical recommendation — all structured through the Decision Cycle. The emphasis is not on memorizing anatomy but on reasoning through it under conditions of clinical ambiguity.

Database Engagement

The database now includes injury history with mechanism, tissue type, and severity; rehabilitation protocol tracking with milestone dates; return-to-play clearance criteria and outcomes; and biomechanical risk profiles. Students query across the full database — pulling performance data (205 lens), training load patterns (325 lens), and injury records (405 lens) — to build integrated clinical assessments. This is the first time students experience the database as a multi-lens professional tool rather than a single-discipline resource.

Skills Developed

Return-to-play protocol reasoning
Biomechanical risk assessment
Rehabilitation outcome tracking
Multi-lens data integration

Decision Cycle reasoning (highest-stakes)

What Students Carry Forward

This course completes the three professional lenses. Students who have taken all three foundational courses have sat in every seat at the sport science team table — data analyst, performance coach, sports medicine clinician. They have queried the same database from three different professional perspectives and made decisions with progressively higher stakes. They are now prepared to design and execute their own investigation (HLSC 499) or step into a professional role (HLSC 490).

HLSC
499

Sport Science Senior Thesis

Independent Research & Athletics Partnership
Professional Lens: Sport Scientist / Applied Researcher

Course Description

The research capstone. Each year, JU Athletics departments (Strength & Conditioning, Athletic Training, Coaching, Sports Performance) submit 3–5 research questions they genuinely want answered. Students select or are matched to projects based on interest and prior coursework strengths. These are not hypothetical exercises — they are questions staff members need resolved to inform their practice.

Students design a study, navigate IRB considerations (most projects use retrospective analysis of existing de-identified data), query the real Athletics database using the same skills they built with simulated data, conduct their analysis, and produce two deliverables: a formal academic thesis (25–35 pages) and a 2–3 page executive summary written for coaches and practitioners. The thesis defense is public; the Athletics presentation is delivered to the staff who posed the question. This dual-audience requirement trains the exact translation skill sport science employers need: the ability to speak both the academic and practitioner languages.

The Simulated-to-Real Bridge

This is the structural payoff of the three-course foundation. Students arrive having already worked with the database schema in simulated form. The querying skills, statistical methods, and Decision Cycle reasoning are identical — only the data is real. The transition feels natural rather than overwhelming because the infrastructure is familiar.

HLSC
490

Sport Science Practicum

Professional Placement & Applied Practice
Professional Lens: Sport Science Practitioner (Integrated)

Course Description

The culminating experience. Students are placed with JU Athletics or external sport science partners as Sport Science Interns, applying the full Decision Cycle framework to real athletes, real coaches, and real operational pressures. This is where three years of progressive skill-building converts into demonstrated professional competence.

Four placement models accommodate different student strengths and site needs:

Placement Model Options

Option A — Single-Sport Embedded (1–3 credits). Student is assigned to one varsity sport for the semester. They attend practices and competitions, collect and enter performance data, run queries for coaching staff, present at team meetings, and shadow athletic trainers during injury assessments. This model builds deep, sport-specific expertise.

Option B — Rotation Model (3 credits). Student rotates through three departments (Analytics/Coaching, Strength & Conditioning, Athletic Training), applying each course's professional lens in sequence. The capstone is an integrated case study on 1–2 athletes seen across all three rotations — demonstrating the multi-lens reasoning the pipeline was designed to build.

Option C — Project-Based Model (2–3 credits). Athletics poses a real research or operational question, and the student conducts the full investigation: proposal, data collection, analysis, written report, staff presentation, and recommendations document. This model mirrors the thesis but with a tighter operational focus.

Option D — Athlete Case Management (2 credits). Student is assigned 3–5 athletes to follow longitudinally. For each athlete, they create a performance dashboard (205 lens), a motor control profile with training recommendations (325 lens), and an injury risk assessment with prevention plan (405 lens). Weekly duties include athlete check-ins, data updates, staff consultations, and progress notes.

Competency Assessment

Generate 5+ actionable reports for staff
Design and implement one training intervention
Complete injury risk profiles for assigned athletes
Present findings to coaches/staff 2+ times
Maintain FERPA compliance throughout
Produce a final portfolio/report

What Students Leave With

Real experience for a resume. A professional network in athletics. Portfolio pieces with actual outcomes. Potential job or graduate assistantship references. And a documented competency record showing they have done the work — not in simulation, not in a classroom exercise, but for real people making real decisions about real athletes.

The Connective Tissue: Decision Cycle & AI Integration

Query
What question are we asking?
Interpret
What does the data show?
Decide
What should we do?
Justify
Why is this the right call?

The Decision Cycle appears in every course, at every level. In HLSC 205, students learn its structure with guided analytical tasks. In EXSC 325, they apply it to training design decisions with moderate ambiguity. In EXSC 405, they use it under clinical pressure where the stakes involve athlete health. In HLSC 499, they execute it independently on a real research question. In HLSC 490, it becomes internalized professional practice — the way they approach every problem in a sport science setting.

AI integration follows the same progression. Early courses scaffold AI use with explicit protocols. Later courses expect students to deploy AI tools fluently and independently, always maintaining the boundary: AI handles computational labor, the student owns the reasoning, the interpretation, and the decision. This is not a policy — it is a professional competency. Graduates will work in environments where AI is standard infrastructure, and the human's value lies in judgment, not calculation.

The shared sport science database is the third unifying element. Same schema, same athlete population (simulated in courses, real in thesis and internship), same query patterns. A student who has built a performance dashboard in HLSC 205 can build one with real data in HLSC 490 without learning a new system. The infrastructure is familiar. Only the stakes change.
