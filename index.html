The Sport Science Decision Lab

A six-course Decision Intelligence pipeline from data literacy to professional placement in sport science
The Sport Science Decision Lab is a Decision Intelligence training pipeline built on a single architectural principle: students should encounter the same data, the same reasoning framework, and the same professional role structure across every course in the sequence. The only variable that changes as they progress is the stakes. What begins as simulated encounters with athlete data in the classroom becomes authentic research with JU Athletics, and ultimately supervised professional practice. Each course trains a distinct professional lens — data analyst, performance coach, predictive analytics director, sports medicine clinician, researcher, practitioner — while the shared Decision Cycle (Query → Interpret → Decide → Justify) and the shared sport science database provide structural coherence across the entire pipeline. The pipeline trains Decision Intelligence: the capacity to close the gap between having data and making decisions with it by mapping critical decisions, blending professional judgment with objective evidence, and defending recommendations under questioning.

The Connective Tissue: Decision Intelligence, Decision Cycle & AI Integration

What question are we asking?
Interpret
What does the data show?
Decide
What should we do?
Justify
Why is this the right call?

Decision Intelligence (DI) is the methodology that transforms data into defensible action. In professional sport science, the data-decision gap — the distance between having analytical output and making a confident recommendation — is where most graduates fail. They can run the analysis but cannot navigate the judgment, communication, and stakeholder trust required to act on it. The Decision Lab trains DI capacity from the ground up, course by course, with progressively higher stakes.

The Decision Cycle appears in every course, at every level. In HLSC 205, students learn its structure with guided analytical tasks. In EXSC 325, they apply it to training design decisions with moderate ambiguity. In EXSC 442, they use it to select, evaluate, and compare predictive models. In EXSC 405, they use it under clinical pressure where the stakes involve athlete health. In HLSC 499, they execute it independently on a real research question. In HLSC 490, it becomes internalized professional practice — the way they approach every problem in a sport science setting.

AI integration follows the same progression. Early courses scaffold AI use with explicit protocols. Later courses expect students to deploy AI tools fluently and independently, always maintaining the boundary: AI handles computational labor, the student owns the reasoning, the interpretation, and the decision. This is not a policy — it is the core of Decision Intelligence: closing the gap between having data and making decisions with it. Graduates will work in environments where AI is standard infrastructure, and the human's value lies in judgment, not calculation.

The shared sport science database is the third unifying element. Same schema, same athlete population (simulated in courses, real in thesis and practicum), same query patterns. A student who has built a performance dashboard in HLSC 205 can build one with real data in HLSC 490 without learning a new system. The infrastructure is familiar. Only the stakes change.
